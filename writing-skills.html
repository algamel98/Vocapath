Paragraph Structure

Every strong paragraph follows a clear structure. Learn the formula for academic and professional writing.

structure

How to Write a Strong Paragraph

Every paragraph = Topic Sentence + Support + Example + Closing. Master this formula.

StructureEvery paragraph = Topic Sentence + Support + Example + Closing
Paragraph Formula
Topic Sentence
Introduce the main idea clearly.
Supporting Sentences
Expand with reasons or evidence (2–3 sentences).
Example
"For example / For instance / To illustrate..."
Closing/Link
Summarize the point or link to the next paragraph.
  • Opinion Essay: "Online education has become increasingly popular in recent years. One of the main advantages is its flexibility, which allows students to learn at their own pace and schedule. For example, working professionals can complete courses in the evening without sacrificing their jobs. This flexibility makes education accessible to a much wider audience. Therefore, online learning represents a significant step forward in democratizing education."
  • Report: "The survey results indicate a clear preference for remote work among employees. Over 70% of respondents stated they would prefer a hybrid model combining office and home work. For instance, many cited improved work-life balance and reduced commuting time as key benefits. These findings suggest that companies should consider implementing flexible work policies. As a result, organizations that adapt to this preference are likely to attract and retain top talent."
  • Formal Email Body Paragraph: "I am writing to request an extension for the project deadline. Due to unforeseen technical difficulties, our team has experienced significant delays in the development phase. Specifically, the integration of the new payment system took longer than anticipated. We would greatly appreciate an additional two weeks to ensure the final product meets your quality standards. This extension would allow us to deliver a more polished and reliable solution."
planning

Essay / Task Plans

Always plan before writing. Spend 5 minutes on structure to save 20 minutes on writing.

StrategyAlways plan before writing; spend 5 minutes on structure
TipFor IELTS/academic tasks: Introduction → 2 body paragraphs → Conclusion
Essay Plan Formula
Introduction
Paraphrase the question + thesis statement (your position)
Body 1
Main point 1 + explanation + example
Body 2
Main point 2 + explanation + example (or counterargument)
Conclusion
Summarize + restate opinion + final thought (no new ideas)
  • Agree/Disagree Essay: Question: "Some people believe that technology has made our lives more complicated. Do you agree or disagree?" | Introduction: Paraphrase + "I strongly disagree with this view." | Body 1: Technology simplifies daily tasks (online banking, GPS, communication) + examples | Body 2: Technology improves efficiency in work and education (automation, e-learning) + examples | Conclusion: Restate disagreement + technology has simplified life overall
  • Discuss Both Views Essay: Question: "Some people think children should start school at an early age, while others believe they should start at seven. Discuss both views and give your opinion." | Introduction: Paraphrase + "This essay will discuss both perspectives before presenting my view." | Body 1: Benefits of early schooling (socialization, academic foundation) + examples | Body 2: Benefits of starting later (maturity, play-based learning) + examples | Conclusion: Both have merit, but I believe starting at age six balances both approaches

Opening & Closing Phrases

The first and last sentences set the tone. Learn how to start and end paragraphs and essays powerfully.

opening

How to Start a Paragraph / Essay

First sentence sets the tone. Never start with "I think that..."

VocabularyFirst sentence sets the tone; never start with "I think that..."
  • Opinion/Argument: It is widely believed that... / There is growing evidence that... / It is undeniable that... / Many experts argue that...
  • Contrast/Concession: While it is true that... / Although some argue that... / Despite the fact that... / Notwithstanding...
  • Topic intro: One of the most significant issues in today's world is... / In recent years, [topic] has become... / The question of whether... has sparked considerable debate.
  • Giving a reason: Due to the fact that... / Given that... / Owing to... / As a consequence of...
Examples
It is widely believed that technology has transformed modern education.
There is growing evidence that remote work increases productivity.
While it is true that social media connects people, it also raises privacy concerns.
Although some argue that exams are stressful, they remain an effective assessment tool.
One of the most significant issues in today's world is climate change.
In recent years, online shopping has become increasingly popular.
Due to the fact that urbanization is accelerating, housing shortages have emerged.
Given that resources are limited, sustainable practices are essential.
closing

How to End a Paragraph / Essay

The closing sentence is your last impression. Make it count.

VocabularyThe closing sentence is your last impression; make it count
  • Summarizing: In conclusion / To summarize / Overall / All things considered / On balance / In short
  • Restating opinion: For the reasons outlined above, I firmly believe that... / It is clear that... / The evidence suggests that...
  • Final thought: It is therefore essential that... / Only by [action] can we [outcome]. / Moving forward, it is crucial to...
  • Linking to next: This leads to the next point, which concerns... / Building on this idea... / Having established this...
Examples
In conclusion, the benefits of renewable energy far outweigh the costs.
To summarize, education plays a vital role in personal and societal development.
Overall, the evidence strongly supports the need for stricter environmental regulations.
For the reasons outlined above, I firmly believe that technology enhances learning.
It is therefore essential that governments invest in public transportation.
Only by working together can we address the challenges of climate change.
This leads to the next point, which concerns the economic implications.
Building on this idea, we can explore alternative solutions.

Transition Words & Connectors

Master the connectors that make your writing flow smoothly and professionally.

connectors

Connectors by Function (Writing)

50+ essential connectors organized by function for academic and professional writing.

  • Adding: Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Additionally, What is more, Besides, Also, Not only… but also, Likewise, Similarly
  • Contrasting: However, Nevertheless, Nonetheless, On the other hand, Despite this, In contrast, Whereas, Although, Even though, Yet, That said, Conversely
  • Cause & Effect: Therefore, Thus, Hence, As a result, Consequently, For this reason, This leads to, Due to, Because of, Owing to, As a consequence
  • Giving examples: For example, For instance, Such as, To illustrate, Namely, In particular, Specifically, To demonstrate, As an illustration
  • Emphasizing: Indeed, In fact, Notably, It is worth noting that, Above all, Particularly, Crucially, Significantly, Especially
  • Sequence: First of all, Secondly, Subsequently, After that, Finally, In the first place, To begin with, Following this, Lastly
  • Concession: Admittedly, It is true that, While it may be argued that, Even if, Granted that, Notwithstanding, Despite the fact that
  • Summarizing: In conclusion, To summarize, Overall, In short, To put it briefly, On balance, In summary, All in all, To sum up
Examples in Context
Online learning offers flexibility. Furthermore, it reduces commuting time.
The policy has benefits. However, it also raises ethical concerns.
The company invested heavily in research. As a result, innovation increased significantly.
Many countries face this issue. For instance, Japan has an aging population.
The findings are significant. Indeed, they challenge previous assumptions.
First of all, we must address the root causes of the problem.
Admittedly, the solution is not without challenges.
In conclusion, the evidence overwhelmingly supports this approach.
The report highlights several issues. Notably, funding remains insufficient.
Technology improves efficiency. Moreover, it enhances communication across teams.

Numbers, Dates & Data in Writing

How to write numbers, percentages, fractions, and dates correctly in formal writing.

TechniqueHow to write numbers, percentages, fractions, and dates correctly in formal writing
Numbers in Writing Formula
Spell out 1–10
"five employees" not "5 employees" (in formal writing)
Use digits for 11+
"15 participants" | "over 200 respondents"
Percentages
"25%" in data/reports | "twenty-five per cent" in formal essays
Fractions in text
"one third of the students" | "a quarter of respondents"
Dates
"15 March 2025" (British) | "March 15, 2025" (American)
Large numbers
"1.5 million" | "approximately 3,000 people"
Charts/Graphs
"The graph shows a sharp rise of 40% between 2020 and 2023."
Ranges
"between 30 and 40 percent" | "from 2019 to 2022"
Examples
According to the chart, sales increased by approximately 35% in the second quarter.
The data indicates that one third of participants preferred option A.
Between January and June 2024, revenue grew from $2 million to $3.2 million.
Roughly a quarter of the respondents reported dissatisfaction with the service.
The unemployment rate fell from 8.5% to 6.2% over a three-year period.
In 2023, the number of users exceeded 1.5 million for the first time.
By the end of the fiscal year, profits had nearly doubled.
The majority — approximately 62% — favored the proposed changes.

Formal Email Writing

Master the structure and phrases for professional email communication in business and academic contexts.

structure

Email Structure & Etiquette

Professional emails have 5 clear parts. Learn the formula.

StructureProfessional emails have 5 clear parts
Email Formula
Subject line
Clear and specific. "Request for Meeting / Follow-up: Job Application / Enquiry Regarding..."
Greeting
"Dear Mr./Ms. [Surname]," | "Dear Sir/Madam," | "To Whom It May Concern,"
Opening line
State your purpose. "I am writing to enquire about... / I am pleased to inform you that..."
Body
1–2 short paragraphs. Be concise and specific.
Closing line
"I look forward to hearing from you. / Please do not hesitate to contact me if you need further information."
Sign-off
"Yours sincerely," (if you used a name) | "Yours faithfully," (if you used Sir/Madam)
